Section 01 — The National Picture

A dramatic fall — but what drove it?

Malaysia recorded 112,355 crimes in 2016. By 2023, that number had fallen to just 52,444 — a reduction most Malaysians may not have noticed amid daily news headlines focused on individual incidents.

The sharpest decline came between 2019 and 2021, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic and the Movement Control Order (MCO). With Malaysians largely confined to their homes, both property and violent crime fell sharply.

"Crime fell fastest during the MCO — but it never fully rebounded after restrictions lifted."

Interestingly, unemployment rose during the same period — reaching 4.64% in 2021. Classical economic theory would predict more crime during hardship, yet crime continued to fall. This challenges simple assumptions about the crime-poverty link.

W.P. K.L. improved the most — but still leads nationally

Despite recording the steepest absolute fall in crime rate, W.P. Kuala Lumpur remains Malaysia's most crime-affected state. Its urban density, large transient population, and high number of commercial targets make it structurally different from other states.

States like Perlis and Kelantan — already low in 2016 — saw smaller absolute improvements, reflecting less room to improve from an already-low baseline.

Note on the rate: Crime rate per 100,000 people accounts for population differences. A large state recording the same raw count as a small one is proportionally safer.

Property crime dominates — but varies widely by state

The vast majority of crime in Malaysia is property crime — theft, break-ins, and vehicle theft. Violent crime makes up a smaller but concerning share. Understanding the breakdown reveals where enforcement and prevention matter most.

Section 04 — Does Poverty Drive Crime?

Wealthier states are not always safer

The relationship between wealth and crime in Malaysia is more nuanced than expected. W.P. Kuala Lumpur — Malaysia's wealthiest and most urban state — also has its highest crime rate. Meanwhile Kelantan, the lowest-income state, has a relatively moderate crime rate.

Why doesn't poverty predict crime here?

Urban density may matter more than poverty. Kuala Lumpur's high crime rate reflects its concentration of people, targets, and economic activity — not its wealth level.

Conversely, Sabah has Malaysia's highest absolute poverty rate (19.7%) but only a moderate crime rate — possibly reflecting stronger community ties and lower urban density.

"Urban concentration, not just poverty, may be the missing variable."
