Section 01 — The National Picture

A dramatic fall — but what drove it?

Malaysia recorded 112,355 crimes in 2016. By 2023, that number had fallen to just 52,444 — a reduction most Malaysians may not have noticed amid daily news headlines focused on individual incidents.

The sharpest decline came between 2019 and 2021, coinciding with the COVID-19 pandemic and the Movement Control Order (MCO). With Malaysians largely confined to their homes, both property crime and violent crime fell sharply.

"Crime fell fastest during the MCO — but it never fully rebounded after restrictions lifted."

Interestingly, unemployment rose during the same period — reaching 4.64% in 2021. Classical economic theory would predict more crime during economic hardship, yet crime continued to fall. This challenges simple assumptions about the crime-poverty link.

Section 02 — Where Crime Happens

Some states are far more dangerous than others

Looking at crime rates per 100,000 people — rather than raw counts — paints a fairer picture. W.P. Kuala Lumpur consistently records the highest rate, while rural states like Perlis and Terengganu remain relatively safe. Use the year selector to watch how each state's profile changed over time.

Which states improved the most? (2016 → 2023)

W.P. K.L. improved the most — but still leads nationally

Despite recording the steepest absolute fall in crime rate, W.P. Kuala Lumpur remains Malaysia's most crime-affected state. Its urban density, large transient population, and high number of commercial targets make it structurally different from other states.

At the other end, states like Perlis and Kelantan — which were already low in 2016 — saw smaller absolute improvements, reflecting less room to improve from an already-low baseline.

Note on the rate: Crime rate per 100,000 people accounts for population differences between states. A state with 5 million people recording 5,000 crimes is proportionally safer than one with 500,000 people recording the same count.

Section 04 — All States at a Glance

Every state tells a different story

While the national trend is clearly downward, the pace and pattern varies dramatically by state. Urban states like Selangor and W.P. Kuala Lumpur start from a high baseline and show steep absolute declines. Rural states like Perlis and Kelantan barely moved — because they were already low.

Sabah and Sarawak — Malaysia's two largest states geographically — show moderate but consistent downward trends, despite having the country's highest poverty rates.

Section 05 — Does Poverty Drive Crime?

Wealthier states are not always safer

The relationship between wealth and crime in Malaysia is more nuanced than expected. W.P. Kuala Lumpur — Malaysia's wealthiest and most urban state — also has its highest crime rate. Meanwhile Kelantan, the lowest-income state, has a relatively moderate crime rate.

Why doesn't poverty predict crime here?

Urban density may matter more than poverty. Kuala Lumpur's high crime rate reflects its concentration of people, targets, and economic activity — not its wealth level. More people and more transactions mean more opportunities for both crime and detection.

Conversely, Sabah has Malaysia's highest absolute poverty rate (19.7%) but only a moderate crime rate — possibly reflecting strong community ties and lower urban density rather than economic safety.

"Urban concentration, not just poverty, may be the missing variable."

This visualisation does not prove causation. It suggests that crime policy in Malaysia needs to look beyond income statistics and consider urbanisation, policing density, and reporting rates.
